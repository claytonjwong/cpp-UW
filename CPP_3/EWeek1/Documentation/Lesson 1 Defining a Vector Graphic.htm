    Lesson One, Part 1: Defining a Vector Graphic

    Required Reading

    The C++ Standard Library 2nd edition: Chapter 15, sections 15.1 through 15.12 (Input/Output Using Stream Classes)

     

    Since we have the luxury of constructing a system "from scratch," we can start in the ideal place: at the very beginning. Starting at zero allows us to get a rounded understanding of the system to be built and the context in which it will be used—two issues that are intimately interdependent. To construct our system, we must know:

    • what vector graphics are; and
    • how and why vector graphics are used.

    Lesson Objective

    By the end of this lesson, you will be able to implement the VectorGraphic and Point classes.

    What Is a Vector Graphic?

    We won't aim for paramount innovation, but instead focus on how we can achieve a simple, straightforward design and maintainable implementation of a product (or suite of products) and all of its future permutations. Our analysis, thus, will be of what exists; it is in the ways we support the design that we might stray from other products. For this reason, we can answer both our initial questions by looking at the commonality among the products that already exist.

    Key Terms
    • bitmap
    • vector
    • structured
    • unstructured
    • flat
    • raw

    For the first question—What are vector graphics?—we can (with research) determine that "vector graphics" is a loose term given to structured graphics that are basically geometrically described. You can think of the terms bitmap and vector as a system of opposites: bitmaps are unstructured (they are effectively a list of colored dots) and vectors are structured (described primarily as shapes that are defined by geometric methods). In this course, we will generally use the terms structured and unstructured to distinguish these sorts of differences, but be aware that the terms flat and raw are often used to refer to unstructured things. Also, be aware that "bitmap" refers to a category of graphics that include more than just those files with "BMP" extensions (JPG, GIF and TIFF files are also effectively bitmaps).

    Having said all this, it might not be entirely apparent that all computer graphics you come into contact with are flattened and unstructured. Sooner or later, things end up on paper or on a screen, and technically at that point they've lost all structure (because both printers and monitors flatten everything down to a matrix of colored dots). This might seem like a nit-picky point, but it does hint at the requirement of most vector graphics systems to be able to "draw" their structured graphics onto an unstructured surface.

    Figure 1.1 begins to illustrate how complicated shapes can be defined in geometrical terms. The remarkable thing about the vector graphic above is that its complexity is built upon just a few straightforward concepts such as points, lines, and curves.

    First-class is a term used to distinguish concepts that are actively represented in a system, versus those that pervade as unspoken. In Lesson Two, we'll look more at the distinction between first-class and second-class concepts, and examine some examples.

    To answer our second question—How and why are vector graphics used?—vector graphics enable the graphic designer (and the software developer) to work with high level concepts of shapes, pen styles, and other concepts that remain "first-class"' throughout the life of the image being created. With primitive drawing tools that do not support vector graphics, drawing a rectangle means merely coloring dots. Once you have drawn the rectangle, you cannot change its dimensions, move it, or modify it in any other way. Effectively, the software no longer treats it as a rectangle once it is drawn.

    This is just like drawing with a pen on paper. If you want to change the rectangle, you erase portions (change them to white or whatever the background color is, using an eraser or perhaps whiteout, in the physical world) and redraw them. This obviously poses quite a problem if your background is not a solid color, or if you're drawing multiple overlapping shapes. In such a case, your pen-and-whiteout drawing might end up looking similar to the drawing shown in figure 1.2.

    Figure 1.2—Changing Shapes without Vectors

    This certainly makes an argument for using vector graphics during the production stage—perhaps working with vector graphics but then, when the image is finished, "flattening" it to a JPG file for a Web site. Of course, Web graphic designers know as well as software developers that the production stage often never ends.

    Producing and editing in terms of vectors but deploying in terms of bitmaps is still common in much Web development, but this stems mostly from a need to work with existing (and fairly ancient, in computer years) technologies. Not all graphic development fields have had to suffer this duality; for-print graphic design, which you might think would be behind the times compared to Web graphic design, has for some time been predominantly vector graphics-based. The benefit to keeping the vector graphics structure until the last possible moment (say, son printing) is twofold:

    • first, the maintenance and production becomes simplified—there aren't two copies (one vector-based and one bitmap-based) of everything; and
    • second, the structured form is closer to the way people think, so it's ideal never to think about the nitty-gritty details of bitmapped graphics.

    What we're talking about here is a principle more far-reaching than our current domain; it's the same reason you are learning object-oriented C++ and not assembly language (ASM) or binary. Translation to "rawer," "flatter" forms might just be necessary due to computer architecture or even physics, but the less translation, the better (whether less is actually going on or whether it's all pushed till the last minute and done in the hardware).

    One argument for this principle is that the higher-level concepts are closer to the way we think. We say and think "That rectangle should be larger" not "Those 700 colored dots should be changed to white and then new ones drawn over here at this point, this point, this point, and that point." When the more natural, high-level concepts are flattened, we sometimes might even need to create technologies to un-flatten them. When text is printed and we need to work with it as text once again, we might use OCR (a character-recognition technology) to do this. If we do, we'll certainly find out that the whole process would have been much nicer had we been able to keep it in digital format the entire time.

    You should recognize that the real power of vector graphics is that they are higher-level, closer to the way we think, and structured (each of which implies the others). An inherent repercussion of defining graphics in terms of connected points, for example, is that they scale and skew easily. Geometrically, we can perform precise stretching and skewing, and then redraw the graphic. This is because a vector graphic is a pure geometrical definition of a shape, with colors, pen styles, and other things applied to it afterwards. In a way, a vector graphic is an instruction on how to draw something, not the actual image. The vector graphic tells whoever is doing the drawing to draw a line from point A to point B with a line width of four and two millimeter dashes. No matter how far apart A and B are, the line will look just as clean and crisp as when it was first conceived (see figure 1.3-A below), not as if it were modified by hand (as in figure 1.2) or by computer (as in figure 1.3-B below).
